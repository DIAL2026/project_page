DIAL: Decoupling Intent and Action via Latent World Modeling for End-to-End VLA

An end-to-end Vision-Language-Action framework that bridges high-level VLM reasoning and low-level motor execution through a differentiable latent intent bottleneck, achieving state-of-the-art performance with 10× fewer demonstrations and robust zero-shot generalization on real-world humanoid robots.

Real-World Deployment Demos

Pre-trained Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have accelerated the development of Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models, yet most end-to-end VLAs treat the VLM as a passive multimodal encoder, mapping features directly to actions. This underutilizes the VLM’s decision-making potential and risks degrading its semantic representations during training.

We introduce DIAL (Decoupling Intent and Action via Latent World Modeling), which bridges high-level reasoning and low-level execution through a differentiable latent intent bottleneck. Rather than directly producing actions, a VLM-based System-2 predicts a future visual subgoal within its native ViT feature space, where alignment with pretrained semantics lets the foresight naturally encode the VLM’s latent intent. This intent serves as the sole interface to a lightweight System-1, which decodes it into precise actions via latent inverse dynamics, strictly anchoring execution to the VLM’s high-level reasoning.

A two-stage paradigm first warms up both systems independently in a shared latent space, then seamlessly transitions to end-to-end optimization that refines the VLM with action-aware gradients while preserving pretrained knowledge. On the RoboCasa GR1 Tabletop benchmark, DIAL establishes a new state of the art with 10× fewer demonstrations than prior methods. By further leveraging heterogeneous human data, DIAL acquires grounded manipulation priors and demonstrates robust zero-shot generalization to unseen objects and novel configurations in real-world humanoid deployments.

Why is this hard?

Translating a VLM’s high-level understanding into precise motor control remains one of the central challenges in embodied AI. Existing approaches fall into two paradigms, each with a fundamental limitation.

Hierarchical planners prompt VLMs to generate text or code plans for a separate low-level controller. While modular, this incurs inter-system latency and introduces a non-differentiable interface that blocks action gradients from refining the VLM’s physical understanding.

End-to-end VLAs directly predict actions from the VLM’s representations. This is fully differentiable, but often reduces the VLM to a passive encoder. Even methods that add auxiliary foresight objectives suffer from a critical weakness—without a strict structural bottleneck, the policy can learn shortcuts that bypass true intent grounding.

Method Overview

DIAL implements a dual-system architecture. System-2 (the “Brain”) is the VLM performing latent world modeling; System-1 (the “Cerebellum”) is a lightweight inverse dynamics policy that turns the predicted intent into motor commands.

System-2

Latent World Modeling

Learnable query tokens are appended to the VLM’s LLM sequence. Their output representations are projected through an MLP head to synthesize the latent intent, trained via MSE alignment with ground-truth ViT features of the future observation.

System-1

Latent Inverse Dynamics

A self-attention module fuses current ViT features with the predicted latent intent. A DiT-based decoder then generates action chunks via flow matching, conditioned on this fused signal and the robot’s proprioceptive state.

Training

Decoupled Warmup → End-to-End

Stage 1: System-2 learns foresight via the world modeling loss; System-1 learns control conditioned on ground-truth future features. Stage 2: System-1 switches to System-2’s predicted intent, enabling action-aware gradients to flow back into the VLM.

Simulation Benchmark Results

We evaluate DIAL on the RoboCasa GR1 Tabletop benchmark (24 tasks: 18 Pick & Place + 6 Articulated). With full training data, DIAL achieves 70.2% average success rate, outperforming the strongest prior method by nearly 15 percentage points. In the few-shot setting (100 trajectories per task, 10% of full data), DIAL reaches 58.3%, already exceeding the best full-data baseline.

Real-World Deployment

We validate DIAL on a humanoid robot across four real-world tasks in two regimes:

  • Cross-embodiment learning: Pick & Place and Pouring, jointly trained on heterogeneous human and robot demonstrations.
  • Multi-stage coordination: Bimanual Handover & Shelving and Trash Collection & Emptying, requiring sequencing several sub-stages with tool use and inter-hand coordination.

DIAL achieves 77.5% in-distribution success and 58.3% out-of-distribution success on cross-embodiment tasks, and demonstrates robust multi-stage coordination with zero-shot transfer to unseen fixtures and surfaces.

Out-of-Distribution Generalization

We evaluate three types of zero-shot generalization: combinatorial (multiple objects requiring disambiguation), distractor robustness (unseen background clutter), and instance-level transfer (novel object geometries). DIAL handles all three without task-specific adaptation.

Multi-Stage Coordination

Bimanual Handover & Shelving (pick → transfer → place) and Trash Collection & Emptying (grasp dustpan → grasp broom → sweep → empty) require coherent phase transitions across sub-stages. DIAL’s latent intent provides reliable guidance across these phases, enabling zero-shot transfer to unseen shelves and tablecloths.

What Does DIAL Learn?

We visualize DIAL’s predicted foresight by projecting latent features onto their top-3 PCA components (mapped to RGB). The predicted foresight closely matches the ground-truth future in task-relevant regions, confirming that System-2 anticipates meaningful state transitions rather than copying the present scene, providing System-1 with a spatially aligned semantic roadmap.
